TEORA

The village of Teora originated as an agricultural, pastoral and military center. In Roman times the tribune of the plebs Milone died there during the civil war between Caesar and Pompey. In a privilege of 1200, granted by Pope Innocent III to Pantaleone, archbishop of Conza, a place is referred to which in the name seems to approach Teora, or Tugurium Biarium. In reality, this village, which in 1464 was already uninhabited (Teora instead existed), indicates the current rural area of ​​Boiara, a locality located in the current municipality of Caposele where you can still see today, next to the church of San Vito, the ruins of the castle. During feudalism, exactly in 1322, a certain Philip was lord of Theora; later (1376) its fiefdom was bought by Count Giacomo Arcuccio and subsequently passed to the Gesualdo family. From 1676 Teora belonged to the Mirelli princes until the abolition of feudalism.
The town was hit by the earthquake of 1694 and the earthquake of 1732. Considerable damage was also caused by the 1980 Irpinia earthquake, following which the town received the gold medal for civil merit in 2005.
